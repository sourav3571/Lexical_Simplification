========================================================================
            LEXICAL SIMPLIFICATION PIPELINE ARCHITECTURE & JUSTIFICATION
========================================================================

Below is the end-to-end data flow showing how datasets are initialized,
how a raw sentence is analyzed, complex words are identified, synonym 
candidates are dynamically generated, and the neural ranking model selects 
the optimal replacement.

============================================================
                 STAGE-BY-STAGE SEQUENCE JUSTIFICATION
============================================================

The ordering of the stages is engineered to optimize computational
efficiency and linguistic accuracy:

1. Stage 0 (Dataset Loading & Configuration) is first:
   - What it does: Scans for dataset files, checks priority (lex_mturk.txt -> BenchLS.txt),
     and loads configurations for downstream components.
   - Why: This sets up all hyper-parameters, thresholds, and paths so that they can be
     referenced consistently across training, evaluation, and inference runs.

2. Stage 1 (Preprocessing) is second:
   - What it does: Resolves grammatical tags (POS tags) and root forms (lemmas).
   - Why: This immediately filters out structural words (e.g. prepositions,
     pronouns, determiners, punctuation). By doing this first, we prevent
     wasting CPU/GPU memory running deep learning models on words that cannot
     be simplified.

3. Stage 2 (Context-Aware CWI) is third:
   - What it does: Evaluates complexity using BERT surprisal, word frequency,
     and length.
   - Why: Typically, only 10% to 20% of words in a sentence are complex.
     By identifying them early, we filter out simple words and ensure the
     expensive Word Sense Disambiguation and Candidate Generation steps are
     only run on actual complex target words.

4. Stage 3 (Word Sense Disambiguation) is fourth:
   - What it does: Finds the exact dictionary meaning (synset) of the word.
   - Why: A word like "bank" has multiple meanings (financial bank vs. river bank).
     Finding the exact meaning in context first ensures that we only generate
     relevant synonyms, completely avoiding out-of-context candidates.

5. Stage 4 (Candidate Generation & Filtering) is fifth:
   - What it does: Pulls candidate synonyms from WordNet and GloVe vectors,
     then filters them.
   - Why: This narrows down a large pool of possible words to a small,
     high-quality list of candidates that are strictly simpler and share the
     same grammatical tag.

6. Stage 5 (Contextual Neural Ranking) is sixth:
   - What it does: Uses the trained neural network to score the candidates.
   - Why: It evaluates each candidate on both semantic fit (meaning preservation)
     and simplicity fit. The final score is computed as:
     Score = Semantic_Fit * Simplicity_Fit
     This acts as an AND gate: a word must be both grammatically correct and
     easier to read.

7. Stage 6 (Substitution & Inflection) is last:
   - What it does: Conjugates the candidate word and replaces the target in the sentence.
   - Why: Candidate generators provide root lemmas (e.g. "requirement" instead of
     "requirements"). Inflecting the word at the very end ensures the replacement
     perfectly matches the original word's number, tense, and capitalization,
     preventing grammatical errors.


============================================================
                 WHY THIS DESIGN IS THE BEST
============================================================

1. Zero Direct Dataset Mapping (Pure Generalization)
   - Candidates are generated dynamically from WordNet and GloVe vector spaces
     rather than a static lookup database. This allows the system to simplify
     completely novel, out-of-vocabulary words.

2. Multi-Gated Feature Fusion
   - Instead of looking at just word frequency or context, the neural ranker
     combines BERT sentence representations, local MLM grammar, and simplicity
     metrics to ensure high fluency.

3. BERT Contextual Redundancy Check
   - The model naturally penalizes words that cause repetitive phrasing (e.g.
     changing "ambiguous" to "unclear" in "ambiguous and unclear" is scored
     lower because of semantic overlap).
